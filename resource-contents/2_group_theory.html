Group/Representation


  1. $G$ is closed under the binary operation: $$ \forall g_1, g_2 \in G \Rightarrow g_1.g_2 = g_3 \in G $$
  2. "." operation is associative: $$ \big( g_1 . g_2 \big) . g_3 = g_1 . \big( g_2 . g_3 \big) $$
  3. $\forall g \in G $ there exists some elements in G; $ \exists e \in G $ such that: $$ e.g = g.e = g $$
  4. \item There exists an unique inverse $ \exists g^{-1}, \forall g $ such that: $$ g^{-1}.g = g.g^{-1} = e $$

It is customary in physics/engineering to think of $G$ as a set of symmetry operations on a set $V$. Sometimes set $V$ is called "realization of space".

Inverse of $ \sigma_A $ is $ \sigma_A $, $ \sigma_B $ is $ \sigma_C $ and $ \sigma_C $ is $ \sigma_C $. $[\pmb{Theorem:} Inverse\ elements\ are\ unique.]$

Subgroups:


If we have a H which is a proper subset of G ($ H \subset G $) then, that itself is a group under the same operation, is a subgroup G.

  • How to identify a subgroup?
$$\forall h_1, h_2 \in H \subseteq G \text{, if } h_1.h_2^{-1} \in \text{H, then H is a subgroup.}$$

Every group has a trivial subgroup which is identity element. Identity element itself is a group. Inverse of identity is itself and if we compose identity with itself it will always give back identity itself.

Closet:


Pick a subgroup $ H \subseteq G $. Consider, an element $ g \in G $ but $ g \notin H $. Then $ g_1H, g_2H, ... $ are called left cosets of H. Likewise $ Hg_i $ are right cosets.

  • Statements: Cosets are not subgroups. $$ \text{Let, } g \notin H. H = \{h_1, h_2, . . . e\} $$ $$ gH = \{ gh_1, gh_2, ... , h_1 \} \not\owns e $$ If there exists an identity in gH then g will be the inverse of an element in H but then that element should have belong to the set G. Hence, there is no identity element in H and it is not a subgroup.
  • Cosets form a partition: If $ g_1 \neq g_2 $ then $ ( g_1H \cap g_2H ) = \varnothing $ or $ g_1 = g_2 $ Suppose there is an element, $$ g_1h_1 \in g_1H = g_2h_2 \in g_2H $$ $$ g_1 = g_2h_2h_1^{-1} = g_2h_3 \text{ [As } h_2h_1^{-1} \in H \text{]} $$ Therefore, $ g_1 $ is in the coset of $ g_2h_3 \in g_2H \Rightarrow g_1 \in g_2H $. But originally $g_1$ and $g_2$ are not in the coset they are outside. That contradicts the initial assumption.

Example:

Let's take a simple sub-group $ \Sigma_A = \{E, \sigma_A\} $. Multiply $ \Sigma_A $ with any element of the group we have seen before. $$ M\Sigma_A = \{M, \sigma_C\} \text{; [It is not a sub-group]} $$ Multiply $ \Sigma_A $ with another element from the group. $$ N\Sigma_A = \{N, \sigma_A\} \text{; [It is also not a sub-group]} $$ We can see there is no intersection between $ M\Sigma_A \text{ and } N\Sigma_A $. Order (numbers of element in each set) of each of these cosets are same as there is no intersection. Order of a particular sub-group is same as the order of its cosets.

Let there is a group G and H is a sub-group of G.

\begin{array}{ |c|c|c|c|c| } \hline & & G & & \\ \hline H & g_1H & g_2H & . . . & g_mH \\ \hline \end{array}
$ n_G = \text{ number of elements in } G $
$ n_H = \text{ number of elements in } H $

$ n_G = n_H + n_{g_{1}H} + n_{g_{2}H} + . . . + n_{g_{m}H} $

$\text{As, cosets form a partition.} $
$ n_H = n_{g_{1}H} = . . . = n_{g_{m}H} $

Hence, $ n_G = Nn_H $; where N is an nonzero positive integer. And $ n_G = Nn_H \geq 1 $ as each group has an identity element.

Therefore, $ \frac{n_G}{n_H} = integer $ [Lagrange's theorem]

Normal Subgroup

If left coset is same as right coset ($ gH = Hg \Rightarrow gHg^{-1} = H $) then H is a normal subgroup.

For normal subgroups, cosets also form a group, which is called ``Quotient Subgroup" $(G/H)$.

Let assume gH and g'H are cosets. Now if we take any two elements: $$ g_1Hg_2H = g_1g_2HH = g_3H \text{ (As left and right cosets are same)}$$

$$ HH = \{e, h_1\}.\{e, h_1\} = \{e.e, e.h_1, h_1.e, h_1.h_1\} = \{e, h_1, h_1, e\} = \{e, h_1\} $$

Product of two coset for a normal subgroup is also another coset. Therefor cosets forms a group for normal subgroups.

Rotation

$ \text{Let, we have a vector } \vec{r} = \begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z \end{bmatrix} $

Under rotation the length of vector remains same. Which means under rotation $ r^Tr = (length)^2 $ is a liner transformation such-that $ (length)^2 $ is invariant. Rotation maps origin int origin and the length of the vector. $$ \vec{r'} = R\vec{r} $$

If we want the $ (length)^2 $ of vector to be invariant then we get: $$ \vec{r'}^{T}\vec{r'} = \vec{r}^{T}R^{T}R\vec{r} $$

Therefore $R^{T}R = I $, hence rotational matrices are orthogonal matrices.

Rotation matrices form a group

Let's take product of two rotational matrix $ R_1 $ and $ R_2 $ $$ R_1R_2 = R_3 $$ To prove if $ R_3 $ is a rotation matrix, we have to show that $ R_3^{T}R_3 = I $ $$ R_3^{T} = R_2^{T}R_1{T} $$ $$ \text{Now, } R_3^{T}R_3 = R_2^{T}R_1{T}R_1R_2 = R_2^{T}IR_2 = R_2^{T}R_2 = I $$

Translation

$ \vec{r'} = R\vec{r} + \vec{a} $ this set also forms a group.

Check for closure property: $$ \vec{r"} = R'\vec{r'} + \vec{a'} = R'(R\vec{r} + \vec{a}) + \vec{a'} = R'R\vec{r} + (R'\vec{a} + \vec{a'}) $$

We can write the operation $ \vec{r'} = R\vec{r} + \vec{a} $ as $ \vec{r'} = (R, \vec{a})\vec{r} $

The composition rule is $ (R', \vec{a'})(R, \vec{a}) = (R'R, R'\vec{a} + \vec{a'}) $

Check for inverse: $$ (R, \vec{a})^{-1} = (R^{T}, -R^{T}\vec{a}) $$

There are two sub-groups of this:

  1. $ (R, 0) = $ rotation
  2. $ (I, \vec{a}) = $ translation
